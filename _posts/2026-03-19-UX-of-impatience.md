I have never started homework when I was supposed to. But I always finished it on time.

People with ADHD are usually told a story about themselves: that they need urgency, that they need pressure, that the only thing that makes them move is a deadline breathing down their neck. I believed this for a long time because it was the only explanation anyone offered. And it's close enough to be convincing.

But it was never quite right for me.

The homework wasn't waiting because I was afraid of it, or avoiding it, or paralysed by it. It was waiting because ten days before it was due, there were other things that needed to happen first. A break I actually deserved after a brutal week. Pre-work for something else that had its own quiet urgency. The slow, invisible accumulation of whatever my brain needed before it was ready to sit down with that particular problem. My brain wasn't procrastinating. It was managing a queue. And somewhere in that queue, the homework had a slot, and when it came up, I was in it completely.

That's the part nobody ever explained to me. Not urgency. Readiness. An internal signal that says "now", not because the world is on fire, but because something has clicked and the conditions are right. Most people either don't have that signal or they've been trained to ignore it. They start things before they're ready because that's what responsible people do. I wait for mine. And I deliver anyway.

The same mechanism that makes starting hard makes finishing almost effortless. When I'm in, I'm genuinely in. Six hours without noticing I'm hungry. I read the entire subject in my engineering at midnight with complete absorption. I also completed The Alchemist in a single afternoon sitting. Problems that stop looking like obstacles and start looking like puzzles with obvious next steps, one after another, almost faster than I can write them down. By the end of it I have so much information moving through my head that I can make any conversation interesting to almost anyone.

A close friend once described this to me using an anime comparison — the kind of character who absorbs the opponent's attacks and grows stronger from them rather than getting knocked back. She was talking about how I learn from people. I don't just receive what someone tells me. I find the thread running through it before they've finished. I connect it to three other things I already know. I hand it back transformed, or I walk away from the conversation carrying everything they spent years figuring out, already being rearranged into something useful. I learned more about how organisations actually work from people I met at jobs than from any book anyone recommended to me. More about product thinking from colleagues I pestered with questions than from any course I enrolled in. The information has to feel live. It has to feel like it's going somewhere. When it does, almost nothing can stop the intake.

This is not a superpower I'm romanticising. It comes with real costs. The executive dysfunction is real. The gap between knowing you need to start something and your body actually starting it is real, and it can be genuinely disabling when the environment doesn't accommodate it. I'm not here to make ADHD sound like a gift you should want.

But here is what living inside this brain for twenty-something years has given me that I couldn't have gotten any other way: an extremely honest read of what human attention actually feels like when you stop performing patience you don't have.

And what I've noticed is that most people are much closer to my experience than the products we build for them assume.

When UX designers talk about user patience, they tend to treat it like a tank of fuel, something you start with and gradually deplete. The job of the product is to not run the tank dry before the user gets what they came for. I think this is the wrong model entirely. Patience isn't a resource. It's a response. People aren't patient or impatient in the abstract. They are patient with things that feel worth their time and completely, immediately unwilling to spend another second on things that don't. The threshold is just usually high enough that they don't leave the moment it's crossed. They stay a little longer. They give you a few more clicks. And then they go, and you mark it as a drop-off event in your analytics and move on.

Nobody asks whether the product gave them a reason to stay in the first five seconds. Whether it showed them something that felt alive before it asked them to do anything. Whether it behaved like a product that understood why they came, or like a product that needed them to explain themselves before it would be useful.

My brain skips the performance of patience entirely. When something doesn't give me a signal fast enough, I'm gone. No deliberation, no second chance, no polite lingering. What you get from me is honest signal: I'm in, or I'm not.

Your users have more tolerance than I do. But less than you think. Nobody is patient. You just think they are.

The thirty-second dare
Open any product you work on. Find the moment a first-time user arrives. Set a thirty-second timer and watch. By the time it runs out, has the product given them something that feels like it was made for them? Not a feature list. Not a progress bar through an onboarding wizard. Not a tooltip asking if they want a tour. Something that makes their brain say "oh, this is mine."

If the answer is no, you are not building for human beings. You are building for the patient, careful, instruction-reading version of human beings that has never actually existed. The real ones are impatient not because they're rude or distracted, but because their time is genuinely limited and their attention is genuinely precious and they are making a continuous, mostly unconscious calculation about whether this thing is worth the next moment of their life.

Design for that person. Not as an edge case you accommodate. As the default you build for.

My brain has ADHD. Your users have lives. The gap between us is narrower than you've been assuming.

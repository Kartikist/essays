Why Enterprise Products Feel So Poor Despite All the Money

I grew up in a house where money was heavy.

Every rupee had a story. If you spent it, you felt it. You didn’t ask, “Does this make me happy?” You asked, “Can we survive if I do this?” That wiring doesn’t just vanish when your salary goes up.

A few months ago, I was at a restaurant. I wanted a masala papad. It cost ₹90.

My brain instantly did the calculation it has always done:
Why would you spend ₹90 on a fried papad with some onion and tomato on top when ₹200 buys a full main course?

I could afford the papad. Objectively. I could afford the entire menu. But the math in my head wasn’t about affordability. It was about a rule I’d carried from childhood: always optimize for the maximum “value” per rupee. The main course wins. The papad loses.

So I didn’t order it.

Later, it hit me: this is exactly how a lot of enterprise products are built.

How scarcity sneaks into software
Look at most internal tools, banking apps, B2B dashboards you’ve used. They feel… poor. Not in terms of functionality. In terms of generosity.

They do the bare minimum.

You can technically complete the task.

You can technically see the report.

You can technically file the ticket.

But there’s no ease. No comfort. No sense that the product is spending anything extra on you.

This is the Masala Papad Effect.

Teams ship the “main course” (the core feature), then look at anything beyond that—delight, clarity, micro-interactions, thoughtful defaults—as a ₹90 papad they can’t justify.

“We don’t have time for that.”
“It doesn’t move the KPI.”
“We’ll do it in v2.”

It’s the same scarcity mindset, just translated into story points and sprints instead of rupees.

Product decisions from a scarcity OS
When a team is operating from scarcity, a few patterns show up:

Checkbox thinking
“Does the feature exist?” becomes more important than “Does the experience feel effortless?”
The ticket gets closed. The release note looks impressive. The user quietly hates it.

Hoarding dev hours
Every small UX improvement is treated as a luxury. A better empty state, a clearer error message, a simple animation that shows progress—these are seen as garnish, not food.
“We can’t afford it this sprint.”

Friction disguised as safety
Extra steps, extra confirmations, extra forms—things that make people feel like they’re walking through security at an airport just to do basic tasks.
It feels “responsible”, but mostly it’s fear.

I recognise this because it’s the same pattern my brain runs with money.

If you grow up with not enough, you train yourself to buy only the main course. No starters, no dessert, no experiment. You tell yourself you’re being rational. But really, you’re afraid.

What a “rich” product looks like
For me personally, “rich” is not a number. It’s the day I can buy the papad without feeling like I betrayed my future.

Translated into product terms, a “rich” product is one that isn’t terrified to spend on the user.

It spends:

A bit more engineering time to remove one unnecessary step.

A bit more design effort to make the first-time experience obvious without a manual.

A bit more copy attention so the user never has to decode what a button does.

It doesn’t throw features at the user to prove how big it is. It quietly absorbs complexity so the user doesn’t have to.

When I look at software I admire, it always has this feeling:
“The team that built this was not panicking. They weren’t counting every pixel like a rupee. They trusted they could afford to be kind.”

Where this leaves me
I still hesitate over stupidly small purchases. I still do the mental ROI of a papad. That script doesn’t disappear overnight.

But as I move into product management, I’m trying to use that same sensitivity differently.

If I’m going to obsess over cost, I want to obsess over the user’s cost:

How many clicks did they pay?

How much confusion did they pay?

How much time did they pay for something that could have been instant?

The Masala Papad Effect is when we optimise so hard for “not wasting” our own resources that we end up wasting the user’s.

I don’t want to build that kind of product.
If anything, I want to be the person in the room who says,
“Relax. We can afford the papad.”
